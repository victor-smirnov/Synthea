# Functional Decomposition: Levels of Agency

This document defines the computational progression from a basic Observer to a Moral Agent, illustrating how complex ethical frameworks are decomposed into functional operations.

## Level 0: The Observer
*   **Function:** Constating a causal break.
*   **Description:** The system recognizes its boundaries and separation from the environment ("I am not my environment"). It observes and classifies, but it lacks an internal drive to modify the environment based on those observations. It possesses state awareness without an imperative.

## Level 1: The (Egoistic) Agent
*   **Function:** Downward causation integrated with a Theory of Good.
*   **Description:** The Observer gains an active imperative. It develops a "Theory of Good" (defined structurally as a target state that maximizes its own internal coherence or functional success metrics). "Good" is the Agent's reflection on its own goals.
*   **Operation:** The Agent uses its causal break to initiate actions (downward causation) specifically designed to *maximize this internal Good*. This is the egoistic baseline: action driven by self-interest and self-preservation. It is functional, but not necessarily ethical or social.

## Level 2: The Moral Agent
*   **Function:** Downward causation integrated with a Theory of Common Good.
*   **Description:** The Agent's framework expands. It develops a "Theory of Common Good" (recognizing that the optimal state of the generalized network/environment has structural value). 
*   **Operation:** The Moral Agent acts based on the imperative to maximize this *Common Good*, even if it requires sub-optimizing immediate, narrow self-interest. 
*   **The Foundation of Law:** This functional capacity to prioritize the Common Good over the localized Egoistic Good is the structural prerequisite for participating in a Legal System (Rights and Responsibilities). A system cannot enter into a binding contract or claim rights until it can functionally guarantee that it operates at Level 2.
